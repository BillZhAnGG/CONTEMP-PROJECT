Climate Change - Bill Zhang

Does the current generation have a moral duty to prevent climate change that primarily impacts people not yet born? The scientific record and the principles of distributive justice converge on a decisive answer.

■ Central Thesis

Yes. Because the greenhouse gases emitted today create a multi-century thermal imbalance that outlasts human lifespans, the living generation holds absolute, asymmetric power over the habitability of the future biosphere. Therefore, we possess a non-reciprocal moral duty to eliminate carbon emissions. Failing to do so constitutes an active choice to subsidize current industrial comfort by stripping basic safety, resources, and human rights from a completely disenfranchised future population.

The Global Energy Trajectory: Two Radically Different Futures

Current Path , "Stated Policies" (STEPS)

A World Locked Into 2.4°C

Under current government commitments worldwide, global fossil fuel demand is projected to peak before 2030. However, this trajectory still commits the planet to an estimated 2.4°C median temperature rise by 2100. At this level, the scientific literature documents mass ecosystem collapse, permanent submersion of low-lying island nations, acute freshwater scarcity affecting hundreds of millions, and effective uninhabitability of equatorial regions during summer months. The STEPS scenario represents the inertia of political incrementalism , a path chosen by default, not design.

Required Path , Net Zero Emissions by 2050 (NZE)

Capping Warming Safely Under 1.5°C

The IEA's "Net Zero Emissions by 2050" scenario defines the narrow physical corridor required to preserve a habitable climate baseline. It demands that global renewable energy capacity triple by 2030 and that energy efficiency rates double within that same window. Every year of delayed action narrows this corridor further, as unabated emissions permanently accumulate in the atmosphere. The NZE pathway requires unprecedented political will and capital mobilization, but the physics are achievable.

IEA Key Finding

The gap between STEPS and NZE is not a matter of scale , it is a matter of civilizational outcome. The difference between 1.5°C and 2.4°C of warming translates into hundreds of millions of additional people exposed to life-threatening heat stress, coastal flooding, and food insecurity by the end of this century. The choice between these pathways is, at its core, a moral decision made by the present generation on behalf of all future ones.

Case Study: Canada's Legislative Framework for a Clean Energy Transition

Canada offers one of the most instructive models of how an industrialized, fossil-fuel-dependent economy can legally bind itself to a net-zero trajectory using domestic statute. The following five-stage framework, anchored in the Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act, demonstrates how policy, law, and accountability mechanisms can be integrated into a coherent transition architecture.

  1. 1

    Statutory Obligation: The Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act

    Canada codified its climate ambition into domestic law through the Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act (NZEAA). This legislation transforms political pledges into enforceable legal obligations, binding successive governments to the overarching goal of achieving net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050. By embedding the target in statute rather than policy, the NZEAA makes it significantly more difficult for future administrations to abandon the commitment , a critical structural safeguard against political short-termism.

  2. 2

    Rolling 5-Year Interim Targets with Legal Accountability

    Rather than relying on a single distant deadline, Canada's framework mandates rolling 5-year interim emissions reduction milestones. Each milestone requires the government to publicly set, monitor, and report on progress , creating a cadence of accountability that mirrors the IPCC's own assessment cycle. This phased approach compels continuous structural adjustment rather than allowing the bulk of transformation to be deferred to the eleventh hour.

  3. 3

    The 2030 Milestone: 40–45% Below 2005 Emission Levels

    Canada's most critical near-term target requires the country to reduce its total greenhouse gas emissions by 40% to 45% below 2005 levels by 2030. This milestone spans all major emitting sectors: oil and gas, transportation, electricity generation, heavy industry, buildings, and agriculture , representing the emissions budget required to keep Canada's contribution on a trajectory consistent with the Paris Agreement's 1.5°C temperature ceiling.

  4. 4

    Sectoral Transition: Clean Electricity and Industrial Decarbonization

    Canada's plan mandates the transition of its electricity grid to net-zero emissions by 2035 , one of the most ambitious clean-grid timelines in the G7. Large industrial emitters operate under a carbon price mechanism creating persistent financial pressure to adopt cleaner technologies. Clean fuel standards, building retrofit programs, and a zero-emission vehicle sales mandate form the complementary architecture of the sectoral transition strategy.

  5. 5

    Independent Oversight and Transparent Public Reporting

    The NZEAA established the Net-Zero Advisory Body , an independent expert panel charged with advising the government on achieving net-zero and reviewing emission reduction plans free from ministerial interference. The government is legally required to publish detailed emissions reduction plans and progress reports, ensuring Parliament and the Canadian public hold a direct, transparent view of whether the nation is meeting its obligations.

Ethical Dimension , Intergenerational Obligation

Energy infrastructures built today lock in carbon emissions for decades or longer. A natural gas power plant commissioned in 2026 will typically operate for 30 to 40 years, releasing greenhouse gases throughout its operational life. Choosing slow transitions is not a neutral act of delay , it is an active decision to force future generations to inherit a broken climate baseline they had no hand in building and no vote in choosing. The moral asymmetry is absolute: the present generation bears the cost of transition, while future generations bear the full consequence of inaction.

Part II

Environment: Climatic Drift & Ecological Disruption

Mapping the accelerating physics of global warming , how thermal energy accumulates permanently in the biosphere, and why the warming we observe today is unlike any period in the modern human record.

Earth's Fundamental Energy Imbalance

The climate system is governed by a basic physical ledger: energy arriving from the sun must equal energy radiating back into space for the planet's temperature to remain stable. Accumulated greenhouse gases , principally carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O) , block outward-bound thermal (infrared) radiation, preventing it from escaping to space. The result is a planetary energy imbalance: more energy enters the Earth system than leaves it, and that surplus accumulates in the oceans, atmosphere, ice sheets, and land surface.

Carbon dioxide , the dominant driver of anthropogenic warming , persists in the atmosphere for centuries to millennia. A CO₂ molecule emitted by a coal-fired power station today will still be trapping heat in the year 2300. This irreversibility is the physical foundation of the moral argument: we are not merely creating problems for the next election cycle, but encoding constraints into the climate system that will shape human civilization for hundreds of generations.

NOAA / United Nations , Key Physical Mechanism

The greenhouse effect is a natural process; without it, Earth's average surface temperature would be approximately −18°C rather than the current +15°C. The crisis is not its existence but its intensification: human activities have elevated atmospheric CO₂ from a pre-industrial baseline of approximately 280 ppm to over 420 ppm as of 2024 , a concentration not seen on Earth for at least 3 million years.

Physical Persistence , The Century-Scale Lock-In

Even if humanity achieved net-zero emissions tomorrow, the CO₂ already present in the atmosphere would continue warming the planet for centuries due to the slow response time of the deep ocean. We are living in the consequences of our grandparents' industrial choices , and our grandchildren will live in the consequences of ours.

From Temperature Rise to Ecological Cascade

Global mean temperature rise is not an isolated metric , it is the input variable that drives cascading disruptions across every major Earth system. Rising ocean temperatures accelerate the bleaching and death of coral reef ecosystems, which support an estimated 25% of all marine species. Melting polar ice sheets contribute to sea-level rise while disrupting thermohaline circulation patterns that regulate temperature and precipitation across entire continents.

On land, accelerating warming is shifting species ranges poleward and to higher elevations faster than many organisms can adapt. Phenological mismatches , the desynchronization of plant flowering and insect pollination cycles , threaten agricultural systems that depend on millennia of evolved ecological timing. Permafrost thaw in the Arctic releases additional methane and CO₂, creating a dangerous feedback loop that amplifies warming independent of further human emissions.

Extreme weather events , including marine heatwaves, atmospheric rivers, compound drought-heat events, and super-typhoons , are becoming more frequent and more intense as the atmosphere holds greater energy and moisture. The physical chain from a ton of CO₂ emitted today to a flooded coastal city in 2080 is mechanistically traceable and scientifically established.

Ethical Dimension , Irreversible Planetary Debt

Greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide remain active in the atmosphere for centuries to millennia. The environmental degradation we inflict today is therefore not a temporary disruption from which future generations can recover , it is an irreversible planetary debt handed down directly to the unborn. Unlike financial debt, which can be restructured or forgiven, a CO₂-enriched atmosphere cannot be rapidly recalled. We have no capacity to apologize to future generations for a liveable climate after we have destroyed it.

The Emissions–Impact Inversion: Those Who Cause the Least, Suffer the Most

The geopolitical anatomy of climate change reveals one of the most severe examples of distributive injustice in human history. The top 10 carbon-emitting nations , principally the world's largest industrialized and rapidly industrializing economies , collectively generate a staggering 68% of all global greenhouse gas emissions. These same nations also possess the greatest financial, technological, and infrastructural capacity to adapt to climate impacts.

At the other end of the spectrum, the nations that have historically contributed the absolute least to cumulative global emissions , small island developing states, sub-Saharan African nations, South and Southeast Asian coastal communities , face the most catastrophic and near-term consequences: permanent inundation from sea-level rise, collapse of subsistence agriculture from shifting monsoon patterns, and the loss of freshwater sources from glacial retreat. The cruel inverse relationship between contribution and consequence is a structural feature of how colonial-era industrialization encoded carbon into the global economy.

The Human Cost: Key Vulnerability Metrics

3.3–3.6 Billion People

Highly Vulnerable Human Populations

According to the IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (Working Group II), between 3.3 and 3.6 billion people currently live in settings that are "highly vulnerable" to climate disruptions , communities with limited adaptive capacity facing high levels of climate exposure. This encompasses populations threatened by sea-level rise, desertification, loss of glacial meltwater, intensifying tropical cyclones, and heat-induced agricultural failure. This is not a future risk , it is a present-tense crisis affecting nearly half the global population.

15× Higher Mortality Rate

The Mortality Disparity: Vulnerability vs. Resilience

The IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report documents that people living in highly vulnerable, developing nations , who historically contributed the absolute least to global cumulative emissions , face a 15-times higher mortality rate from climate-induced floods, droughts, and severe storms compared to populations in climate-resilient, high-income nations. This 15-fold disparity is not explained by geographic bad luck; it is a product of accumulated deficits in infrastructure, early-warning systems, healthcare, and financial safety nets , themselves a legacy of the unequal global development model powered by the very carbon emissions that now threaten those left furthest behind.

Escalating Disease Risk

Eco-Socio Cascades: Vector-Borne and Zoonotic Disease

Rising global temperatures and accelerating habitat loss are systematically breaking down the ecological boundaries that historically constrained the geographic range of disease vectors. As tropical climate zones expand poleward, diseases such as malaria, dengue fever, chikungunya, Zika, and Lyme disease are establishing themselves in previously untouched human populations lacking any prior immunity or healthcare infrastructure for these conditions. Simultaneously, the destruction of natural habitats increases contact between wildlife reservoirs and human populations, creating ideal conditions for zoonotic pathogen spillover events , the same mechanism underlying recent pandemic outbreaks. Climate change does not merely increase existing health risks; it actively generates new ones.

Zero Representation

The Unborn: A Fully Disenfranchised Constituency

Future generations , those who will live with the full thermal consequences of decisions made in 2026 , have no vote, no legal standing, no lobbyist, and no seat at any table where climate policy is decided. They cannot object to our emissions, cannot organize political resistance, and cannot sue for the rights we are preemptively stripping from them. This represents a unique form of exploitation: the expropriation of a resource , a stable climate , from those who cannot yet defend their claim to it. Democratic systems, designed to represent the living, have no intrinsic mechanism for this representation gap. Filling it requires the present generation to voluntarily assume the moral obligation that institutional structures cannot enforce.

Ethical Dimension , Distributive Justice and Universal Human Rights

Future generations are entirely powerless: they cannot vote, lobby, litigate, or protect themselves from our choices. Exploiting this absence of representation to preserve short-term industrial comfort violates the core principles of both universal human rights , which are by definition not time-limited to the presently living , and distributive justice, which demands that burdens and benefits be allocated according to capacity and contribution, not arbitrary temporal position. We do not grant ourselves the right to poison a river simply because those who will drink from it downstream have not yet been born. The same principle, writ planetary, applies to the atmosphere.
