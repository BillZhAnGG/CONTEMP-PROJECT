Climate Change - Bill Zhang

Does the current generation have a moral duty to prevent climate change that primarily impacts people not yet born? The scientific record and the principles of distributive justice converge on a decisive answer.

■ Central Thesis

Yes. Because the greenhouse gases emitted today create a multi-century thermal imbalance that outlasts human lifespans, the living generation holds absolute, asymmetric power over the habitability of the future biosphere. Therefore, we possess a non-reciprocal moral duty to eliminate carbon emissions. Failing to do so constitutes an active choice to subsidize current industrial comfort by stripping basic safety, resources, and human rights from a completely disenfranchised future population.

The Global Energy Trajectory: Two Radically Different Futures

Current Path , "Stated Policies" (STEPS)

A World Locked Into 2.4°C

Current commitments project fossil fuel demand peaking before 2030, but still lock in an estimated 2.4°C rise by 2100 , bringing ecosystem collapse, island submersion, and acute freshwater scarcity. A path chosen by default, not design.

Required Path , Net Zero Emissions by 2050 (NZE)

Capping Warming Safely Under 1.5°C

The NZE pathway requires renewable capacity to triple and efficiency rates to double by 2030. Every year of delay narrows the corridor as emissions accumulate. Politically demanding , but physically achievable.

Case Study: Canada's Legislative Framework for a Clean Energy Transition

Canada's Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act shows how a fossil-fuel-dependent economy can legally bind itself to net-zero , integrating policy, law, and accountability into a coherent transition framework.

  • Statutory Obligation: The Net-Zero Emissions Accountability Act

    The NZEAA transforms political pledges into enforceable law, binding all successive governments to net-zero by 2050 , making abandonment far harder than a policy reversal.

  • Rolling 5-Year Interim Targets with Legal Accountability

    Legally mandated 5-year milestones require the government to publicly set, monitor, and report targets , compelling continuous adjustment rather than last-minute action.

  • The 2030 Milestone: 40–45% Below 2005 Emission Levels

    Canada must cut emissions 40–45% below 2005 levels by 2030, spanning oil and gas, transport, electricity, industry, buildings, and agriculture , the budget needed to stay within 1.5°C.

  • Sectoral Transition: Clean Electricity and Industrial Decarbonization

    The grid must reach net-zero by 2035 , among the G7's most ambitious targets. Carbon pricing, clean fuel standards, building retrofits, and a zero-emission vehicle mandate complete the sectoral picture.

  • Independent Oversight and Transparent Public Reporting

    The Net-Zero Advisory Body , an independent expert panel , reviews plans free from ministerial interference. Legally required progress reports keep Parliament and the public informed.

Part II

Environment: Climatic Drift & Ecological Disruption

How thermal energy accumulates permanently in the biosphere , and why today's warming is unlike anything in the modern human record.

Earth's Fundamental Energy Imbalance

Greenhouse gases , CO₂, CH₄, N₂O , block outbound thermal radiation, creating a planetary energy imbalance: more energy enters than leaves. The surplus accumulates in oceans, atmosphere, and ice sheets.

CO₂ persists for centuries to millennia. A molecule emitted today still traps heat in 2300 , encoding constraints into the climate system that will shape civilization for hundreds of generations.

NOAA / United Nations , Key Physical Mechanism

Without the greenhouse effect Earth would be −18°C. The crisis is its intensification: CO₂ has risen from 280 ppm pre-industrial to >420 ppm in 2024 , a level unseen for 3 million years.

Physical Persistence , The Century-Scale Lock-In

Even at net-zero tomorrow, existing CO₂ would keep warming the planet for centuries. We live in our grandparents' emissions , our grandchildren will live in ours.

From Temperature Rise to Ecological Cascade

Temperature rise drives cascading disruptions across every Earth system. Rising ocean temperatures bleach coral reefs supporting 25% of marine species; melting ice sheets raise sea levels and disrupt circulation patterns regulating continental climates.

On land, species ranges shift poleward faster than ecosystems adapt. Permafrost thaw releases methane, amplifying warming further. Marine heatwaves, atmospheric rivers, and super-typhoons intensify as the atmosphere holds more energy , the chain from today's emissions to tomorrow's flooded cities is scientifically established.

The Emissions–Impact Inversion: Those Who Cause the Least, Suffer the Most

The top 10 emitting nations produce 68% of global greenhouse gases and hold the greatest capacity to adapt. Meanwhile, small island states, sub-Saharan Africa, and South Asian coastal communities , who contributed the least , face permanent inundation, agricultural collapse, and freshwater loss. This inverse relationship between cause and consequence is a structural feature of how industrialization encoded carbon into the global economy.

The Human Cost: Key Vulnerability Metrics

3.3–3.6 Billion People

Highly Vulnerable Human Populations

The IPCC AR6 finds 3.3–3.6 billion people in "highly vulnerable" settings , exposed to sea-level rise, desertification, glacial meltwater loss, and crop failure. This is a present-tense crisis affecting nearly half the global population.

15× Higher Mortality Rate

The Mortality Disparity: Vulnerability vs. Resilience

Developing nations , least responsible for cumulative emissions , face a 15× higher mortality rate from floods, droughts, and storms. The disparity stems from infrastructure deficits that are themselves a legacy of the carbon-powered development model now threatening them most.

Escalating Disease Risk

Eco-Socio Cascades: Vector-Borne and Zoonotic Disease

Warming breaks down ecological boundaries that constrained disease vectors. Malaria, dengue, and Lyme disease expand into populations with no prior immunity. Habitat destruction increases wildlife-human contact, enabling zoonotic spillovers , the same mechanism behind recent pandemics. Climate change doesn't just worsen existing risks; it creates new ones.

Zero Representation

The Unborn: A Fully Disenfranchised Constituency

Future generations have no vote, no legal standing, and no seat where climate policy is decided. They cannot object, organize, or sue. Democratic systems have no mechanism for this gap , filling it requires the present generation to voluntarily assume an obligation no institution can enforce.
